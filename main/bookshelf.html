The Bhagavad Gita, often referred to as the Gita, is a 700-verse Hindu scripture that is part of the epic Mahabharata. It contains a conversation between Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna on a variety of philosophical issues. Faced with a fratricidal war, a despondent Arjuna turns to his charioteer Krishna for counsel, resulting in a profound dialogue that presents the essence of Hindu philosophy and wisdom.

The Mahabharata Context

The Battlefield of Kurukshetra

The Bhagavad Gita occurs in the Bhishma Parva of the Mahabharata and comprises 18 chapters from the 25th through the 42nd. The Mahabharata epic describes the conflict between two groups of cousins - the Kauravas and the Pandavas - culminating in the Kurukshetra War.

On the first day of the war, the great warrior Arjuna asks his charioteer Krishna to position his chariot between the two armies. Seeing his relatives, teachers, and friends in the opposing army, Arjuna is overcome with grief and moral dilemma about fighting his own kin. This moment of crisis triggers the dialogue known as the Bhagavad Gita, where Lord Krishna, who reveals himself as the Supreme Being, imparts divine wisdom to Arjuna about duty (dharma), righteous action, and spiritual knowledge.
